The Terrestrial  Planets

  1. Mercury
    • Almost Tidally Locked 3:2 spin.
    • Days are longer then years.
    • 0.38G or 3.7 m/s2.
    • Surface Temperature ranges from -279°F to 801°F.
    • Atmospheric Pressure is 10-12 bar.
  2. Venus
    • Runaway Greenhouse Effect.
    • Venus spins backwards, or clockwise.
    • 0.91G or 8.87 m/s2.
    • Surface Temperature ranges from 820°F to 900°F.
    • Atmospheric Pressure is 95 bars.
  3. Earth
    • Days are slowly getting longer.
    • Gravitational Fields change based on location.
    • 1.00G or 9.81 m/s2.
    • Surface Temperature ranges from -129°F to 136°F.
    • Atmospheric Pressure is 1.01 bar.
  4. Mars
    • Very Thin Atmosphere.
    • Sunsets on Mars are blue.
    • 0.38 G's or 3.72 m/s2.
    • Surface Temperature ranges from -125°F to 23°F.
    • Atmospheric Pressure is 0.006 bar.

Mars Information

Mars is the fourth planet from the sun. Befitting the Red Planet's bloody color, the Romans named it after their god of war. In truth, the Romans copied the ancient Greeks, who also named the planet after their god of war, Ares.

Other civilizations also typically gave the planet names based on its color — for example, the Egyptians named it "Her Des-her," meaning "the red one," while ancient Chinese astronomers dubbed it "the fire star."

The Local Group

The Local Group is a large cluster of galaxies & gravitationally dense space that is gravitationally bound to us. As the Universe continues to expand rapidly all other galaxies outside of our Local Group will be lost to us forever! This is because the expansion of the Universe is faster then we could ever hope to travel & by the time we ever reached the edge of the Local Group all other celestial bodies will be long gone.

Relativity

If you clicked reveal answers above you've seen what the oldest star is. However due to relativity time can get a bit wonky. The best way to explain it is imagine an unbreakable string. Now imagine a particle of light is permanently attached to this string. The time it takes for light to reach the start of the string to the end always has to be the same. Since large celestial bodies warp the fabric of space, certain parts of this string will also warp around it. This makes the length of this string longer. Since light is constant, i.e it can not accelerate nor slow down, how does it manage to arrive at the end of the this elongated string at the same time as it would the regular one? This is what Relativity is. Regions wherein this 'string' is elongated & light has a longer path to take before reaching its destination have an effect called time dilation. Time moves slower to accomadate this effect. A minute near the event horizon of a black hole could be thousands of years for someone just slightly farther away!

Calculating Time Dilation

The following equations are used to calculate time dilation! Satellites orbiting the Earth actually use this equation to make micro adjustments to their internal clocks. This is because even with Earth's 'relatively' weak gravitational field, space-time is still stretched; ever so slightly!

For a more personal example, let's say you have a friend traveling for 20 years at 50% light speed (which is around 150,000,000 m/s). Plugging in those numbers into the calculators below will tell you (after converting seconds to years) how much time for them has passed; relative to your 20 years on Earth!

If you've ever seen Interstellar you'll know how gravity can affect time dilation as well! Lets use the supermassive blackhole Ton-618 for this next example. With an estimated mass of 66 billion solar masses, or 1.31 x 1041kg & a Schwarzschild radius of 1300 astronomical units (that's around 195 billion km, or for calculating purposes; 195000000000000 m), if you were as close as possible to this blackhole you can plug in the numbers to see how long 1 year for you would be for the rest of the universe!
  1. Special Relativity Time Dilation Equation:

    t' = t √(1 - V2 / c2)
    • t' = Dilated Time or the time of the object
    • t = Stationary Time or the time of the observer
    • V = Velocity or speed of the object
    • c = Speed of Light in a vacuum

  2. Gravitational Time Dilation:

    T = T' / √(1 - (2GM / rc2))
    • T = Dilated Time or time passed for the object
    • T0 = Stationery Time or time passed for the observer
    • G = Gravitational Constant
    • M = Mass of the Gravitational Body
    • r = Distance from the center of the Gravitational Body
    • c = Speed of Light in a vacuum
